Focus

This Wikiblog explores how transposable elements, mobile DNA sequences making up nearly half the human genome [1, 2], can trigger the cGAS-STING innate immune pathway, driving autoimmune disease, post-COVID-19 complications, and neuroinflammation [3]. The sequential sections from molecular mechanism to therapeutic innovation incorporate developments from 2024 onwards that were not covered in my Year 3 courses.

Rationale & Structure

During Year 3, three courses were fundamental to my deeper understanding of the human genome and the immune system. Firstly, Clinical Immunology and Haematology 3A explored innate and adaptive immunity, including the acute inflammatory response, mechanisms of autoimmune disease, and the therapeutic landscape for SARS-CoV-2, for which I produced a Wikiblog on COVID-19 immunotherapies as part of a group in-course assessment. Secondly, Medical Microbiology analysed how viruses and other pathogens exploit and evade host defences and examined different therapeutic approaches. Finally, Molecular Genetics introduced me to transposable elements (TEs), the “jumping genes” that constitute nearly half our genome (Lectures 15-16 in Appendix). At the time, I did not have additional information to link TEs to immunity.

This changed after Year 3 when I learned about the cGAS-STING signalling pathway through independent reading. cGAS-STING is an innate immune sensor that detects cytoplasmic DNA and triggers the production of type I interferons, IFN-α and IFN-β [4, 5]. I was intrigued by its dual role, serving both as a frontline antiviral defence and as a driver of autoimmune pathology when chronically activated by self-derived DNA [6]. Critically, one major source of self-derived DNA is the TEs I encountered in Molecular Genetics, providing a mechanistic bridge between my separate courses.

This association is now supported by a growing body of evidence, including genetic studies of interferonopathies like Aicardi-Goutières syndrome [7, 8], pharmacological studies showing HIV reverse transcriptase inhibitors reduce LINE-1-driven interferon signalling in lupus [9], and the landmark finding that LINE-1 reactivation drives type I interferon production in senescent cells [10]. These findings establish that TEs are not merely ‘genomic parasites’ but also active participants in innate immune regulation.

1. The cGAS-STING Pathway - A Key Cytosolic DNA Sensor

1.1 Discovery and Significance

The innate immune system’s ability to detect cytoplasmic/cytosolic DNA (cytoDNA) has been acknowledged since the early 2000s, but the identity of the actual sensor remained elusive for nearly a decade. The identification of STING (Stimulator of Interferon Genes) as an endoplasmic reticulum (ER)-resident adaptor protein marked a critical advancement, demonstrating its role in linking cytoDNA detection to type I interferon (IFN) production [11]. However, STING itself does not bind DNA directly, but serves as a signal transduction adaptor rather than a pattern recognition receptor [12].

The breakthrough arrived in 2013 with two successive publications in Science. Sun et al. employed biochemical fractionation and mass spectrometry on the murine fibrosarcoma cell line L929 to identify cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS), a nucleotidyltransferase that directly binds cytoplasmic double-stranded DNA and catalyses synthesis of the second messenger 2’3’-cyclic GMP-AMP (cGAMP) [4]. In the companion publication, Wu J et al. independently characterised cGAMP as the endogenous dinucleotide that activates STING, thereby establishing the complete sensor-adaptor-effector axis [13]. Gao et al. further demonstrated that cGAS serves as an innate immune sensor of HIV and other retroviruses, directly connecting the pathway to retroviral biology, extending to the genome’s own retroviral-like elements [14].

Remarkably, this pathway appears to be ancient. Kranzusch PJ et al. identified functional cGAS and STING homologues in the sea anemone Nematostella vectensis, an organism that diverged from humans over 500 million years ago [15]. The anemone cGAS produces 3’3’-cGAMP rather than the mammalian 2’3’-cGAMP, indicating that while the core detection mechanism is deeply conserved across metazoa, the signalling specificity has continued to evolve [15]. This deep evolutionary conservation underscores the fundamental importance of cytosolic DNA sensing in host defence [16, 17].

1.2 Mechanism of Action

According to comprehensive studies, the cGAS-STING signalling cascade involves five mechanistically distinct steps [3, 18]:

Step 1 - DNA Detection

cGAS binds cytosolic dsDNA in a sequence-independent manner, recognising the sugar-phosphate backbone rather than specific nucleotide sequences [4]. This means cGAS detects any dsDNA in the cytoplasm, regardless of its origin, a feature that is simultaneously its greatest strength as a broad-spectrum pathogen sensor and its principal vulnerability in autoimmune disease. Structural studies have revealed that cGAS dimers multimerise on DNA and form liquid-liquid phase-separated biomolecular condensates, enabling switch-like activation kinetics and selectivity for long, accessible DNA over short fragments or nucleosome-wrapped chromatin [19]. This phase separation concentrates the enzyme and reactants to enhance STING-dependent interferon responses while maintaining a threshold against spurious activation by small amounts of self-DNA [20].

Step 2 - Second-messenger Synthesis

Upon DNA binding, cGAS undergoes a conformational change that activates its nucleotidyltransferase domain, catalysing the synthesis of 2’3’-cyclic GMP-AMP (cGAMP) from ATP and GTP [3, 21]. cGAMP functions as a second messenger that binds to and activates STING and contains a distinct combination of 2’-5’ and 3’-5’ phosphodiester bonds, structurally differentiating it from bacterial cyclic dinucleotides (CDNs), which contain only 3’-5’ linkages [3]. This distinction is functionally significant since 2’3’-cGAMP binds mammalian STING with much higher affinity than bacterial CDNs, suggesting co-evolution between cGAS and STING [3].

Step 3 - STING Activation and Trafficking

The cGAMP binds to the STING homodimer on the ER, inducing dramatic conformational changes. Cryo-electron microscopy structures revealed a ~180° rotation of the STING ligand-binding domain, which promotes oligomerisation [22]. The oligomerised STING then translocates from the ER to the ER-Golgi intermediate compartment (ERGIC) and then to the Golgi apparatus via COP-II vesicles [23]. At the trans-Golgi network, palmitoylation at cysteine residues C88 and C91, mediated by ZDHHC-family palmitoyltransferases, is necessary for STING clustering in lipid rafts and full signalling competence [13, 24]. Notably, it was demonstrated that STING-induced autophagy at the ERGIC represents its most evolutionarily ancient function, predating the interferon signalling that dominates in mammals [23].

Step 4 - Downstream Signalling

Activated STING recruits TANK-binding kinase-1 (TBK1) through a conserved PLPLRT/SD motif, triggering two parallel signalling cascades [25]. First, TBK1 phosphorylates Interferon Regulatory Factor-3 (IRF3), a transcription factor which dimerises and translocates to the nucleus to initiate transcription of type I interferons (IFN-α, IFN-β), while STING activates NF-κB, driving the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, including TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β [18]. This dual effect indicates that cGAS-STING activation simultaneously establishes an antiviral state through IFN and recruits inflammatory cells through cytokines [5, 25].

Step 5 - Effector Response and Resolution

Secreted type I interferons act in a paracrine manner, binding the IFNAR1/IFNAR2 receptor complex on neighbouring cells that weren't originally affected and activating the JAK-STAT signalling cascade [26]. JAK1 and TYK2 phosphorylate STAT1 and STAT2, which form the trimeric ISGF3 complex with IRF9 and translocate to the nucleus to induce expression of multiple interferon-stimulated genes (ISGs) [3, 18]. ISGs include OAS/RNase L, responsible for viral RNA degradation, PKR (Protein Kinase R), which suppresses translation, and Mx proteins, which block replication. Collectively, these establish a broad antiviral state, amplifying the response across the local tissue [26].

Crucially, the pathway incorporates negative feedback to prevent excessive activation. Following signalling, STING is trafficked to lysosomes and degraded via p62-mediated autophagy [23], while the phosphodiesterase ENPP1 cleaves extracellular cGAMP to limit paracrine spread [27]. Additionally, the cytoplasmic exonuclease TREX1 degrades DNA to remove the cGAS ligand [7, 28]. The implication of this resolution machinery is demonstrated by Aicardi-Goutières syndrome, in which loss-of-function mutations in TREX1 and related nucleases drive constitutive cGAS-STING activation and severe type I interferonopathy, providing a direct genetic link between impaired pathway resolution and autoimmune disease [8]

1.3 Autoimmunity - When Self-DNA Triggers the Alarm

Although the cGAS-STING pathway evolved to detect foreign DNA from invading pathogens, the critical concern is that cGAS does not appear to distinguish foreign DNA from self-derived DNA [29]. Under normal physiological conditions, genomic DNA is rigorously confined to the nucleus and mitochondria [30, 31]. Nevertheless, multiple pathological mechanisms lead to aberrant accumulation of self-DNA in the cytoplasm [32, 33].

Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) Release

Cellular stress, viral infection, or metabolic dysfunction damages mitochondria, causing mtDNA to leak through permeabilised outer membranes into the cytoplasm [34]. It was revealed that impaired mitophagy, that is, the selective autophagy of damaged mitochondria, dramatically amplifies mtDNA-driven cGAS-STING activation and type I IFN responses [35]. Apoptotic caspases normally prevent this by maintaining mitochondrial integrity, but when caspase function is compromised, mtDNA-driven inflammation becomes constitutive, leading to a dangerous self-reinforcing cycle where inflammation damages mitochondria, resulting in mtDNA leakage into the cytosol and subsequent cGAS-STING activation [30, 31]. This drives additional inflammation and exacerbates mitochondrial damage [30].

Neutrophil Extracellular Traps (NETs)

Neutrophils release chromatin-based extracellular webs to trap and kill pathogens. In Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE), however, excessive NETosis fills the extracellular space with self-DNA complexed with antimicrobial proteins [9, 36]. It has been demonstrated that NET-derived oxidised mtDNA is highly interferogenic and that impaired DNase I activity in SLE patients prevents efficient NET clearance, perpetuating chronic immune activation and inflammation [36]. Similarly, NET-associated proteins were implicated in activating the NLRP3 inflammasome in SLE macrophages, creating an additional inflammatory axis [37].

Clearance defects

When apoptotic cells are not efficiently cleared by efferocytosis, that is the clearance of apoptotic cells by professional and non-professional phagocytes [38], their DNA persists and can be internalised by neighbouring cells or antigen-presenting cells (APCs) [28]. It was revealed that a deficiency in DNase1L3, which specifically digests chromatin in apoptotic cell microparticles, induces spontaneous autoimmunity in mice, suggesting that failure to clear endogenous DNA drives autoimmune pathology [39]. It was also shown that apoptosis-derived membrane vesicles drive cGAS-STING activation and enhance type I IFN production in SLE [40].

Transposable Element Activity

The fourth and perhaps most unrecognised source of cytoplasmic self-DNA comes from the genome’s own mobile genetic elements [28]. When epigenetic silencing of retrotransposons fails, their reverse transcription generates cytoplasmic DNA intermediates that are indistinguishable from viral replication products, a phenomenon explored in detail in Section 2 [6].

2. Transposable Elements - Genomic Passengers As Immune Triggers

2.1 Overview and Classification

The landmark Human Genome Project revealed that TEs constitute at least 45% of the human genome, significantly more than the approximately 1.5% encoding proteins [41]. TEs are classified into two major classes based on their transposition mechanism [42] (Figure 3).

A. Class I retrotransposons use a “copy-and-paste” mechanism via an RNA intermediate that is reverse-transcribed back into DNA. These include:

  • Long Interspersed Nuclear Elements (LINEs), of which LINE-1 (~21% of the genome, ~850,000 copies) is the most significant
  • Short Interspersed Nuclear Elements (SINEs), principally Alu elements (~13%, ~1.5 million copies)
  • Long Terminal Repeat (LTR) elements, including human endogenous retroviruses (HERVs, ~8%, 450,000 copies).

    • B. Class II DNA transposons use a “cut-and-paste” mechanism (~3%) but are no longer active in humans and represent genomic “fossils” [41, 43] (Figure 3)

    LINE-1 is of particular significance because it is the only autonomously active TE in the human genome. It encodes two proteins: ORF1p, an RNA-binding protein that forms trimeric complexes, and ORF2p, which possesses both endonuclease and reverse transcriptase (RT) activities, thereby providing the complete molecular machinery for independent retrotransposition through target-primed reverse transcription (TPRT) [44]. LINE-1 also mobilises non-autonomous SINEs, particularly Alu elements, in trans, thereby driving the retrotransposition of elements beyond itself [42]. Under normal conditions, TEs are epigenetically silenced through a multi-layered defence that includes DNA methylation at CpG sites in the LINE-1 5’UTR promoter, repressive histone modifications, such as H3K9me3, and the binding of chromatin regulators, such as SIRT6 [45]. Therefore, impairment of these defences leads to immunologically significant consequences.

    HERVs, although incapable of autonomous retrotransposition due to accumulated mutations, remain transcriptionally active and retain structural features resembling those of their retroviral ancestors, including gag, pol, and sometimes env genes. Their expression increasingly appears to modulate innate immune responses, with particular relevance to neuroinflammation [46].

    2.2 The “Viral Mimicry” Mechanism

    The bridge between TE biology and autoimmune pathology has been proposed to operate through a form of viral mimicry, in which endogenous retroelement-derived nucleic acids are processed by innate immune pathways that normally respond to viral infection. Early evidence for this concept demonstrated that failure to degrade endogenous retroelement-derived DNA in Trex1-deficient systems was associated with activation of a cell-intrinsic interferon response and autoimmune pathology [28].

    The proposed link between LINE-1 derepression and autoimmune pathology can be described as the following sequence of events:

  • Loss of epigenetic silencing permits LINE-1 reactivation

    • Under normal conditions, LINE-1 elements are repressed by epigenetic mechanisms, including DNA methylation. However, when this repression is reduced or lost, LINE-1 transcription may be reactivated. Experimental work has demonstrated that hypomethylation of a human LINE-1 promoter was associated with activation of LINE-1 sense transcription in human bladder cancer cell lines [47].

  • Full-length LINE-1 RNA is translated and forms a ribonucleoprotein complex

    • Active human LINE-1 elements contain an internal RNA polymerase II promoter within the 5′ untranslated region (UTR) and encode two proteins, ORF1p and ORF2p, which are required for retrotransposition [48]. It was revealed that these proteins assemble together with LINE-1 RNA to form a ribonucleoprotein complex, which is proposed to represent a key intermediate in the retrotransposition cycle [48].

  • Reverse transcription generates LINE-1-derived DNA intermediates

    • Retrotransposition of LINE-like elements is understood to occur through target-primed reverse transcription, a mechanism first described for non-LTR retrotransposons. [49]. In mammalian systems, reverse-transcribed LINE-1 DNA species have been reported to accumulate under certain conditions, including cytoplasmic LINE-1 cDNA in senescent cells, where it was proposed to contribute to inflammatory signalling [10, 28].

  • Failure to clear retroelement-derived DNA can promote innate immune activation

    • Similarly, endogenous retroelement-derived DNA has been shown to accumulate in the absence of the exonuclease Trex1, which is associated with activation of a cell-intrinsic interferon response and autoimmune pathology [28]. These findings support the idea that inefficient clearance of retroelement-derived nucleic acids could permit them to act as immunostimulatory ligands [28].

  • Persistent cytoplasmic DNA may engage innate DNA-sensing pathways

    • cGAS functions as a cytosolic DNA sensor and synthesises 2′3′-cGAMP in response to DNA [4]. STING is required for intracellular DNA-triggered type I interferon signalling, suggesting that self-derived cytoplasmic DNA species could feed into and activate this pathway [50].

  • STING signalling promotes downstream interferon responses

    • Downstream of STING, TBK1-dependent phosphorylation of IRF3 has been reported to promote the induction of type I interferon genes. This signalling cascade resembles the antiviral programme normally activated during infection [51].

    Chronic activation may contribute to inflammatory or autoimmune pathology

    Taken together, these studies support a model in which LINE-1 derepression may generate reverse-transcribed DNA intermediates that, when not efficiently cleared, contribute to activation of the cGAS-STING-TBK1-IRF3 interferon axis [10, 21, 28, 50, 51]. This provides a plausible mechanistic explanation for how transposable element activity could contribute to immune dysregulation through viral mimicry [28].

    Several conditions have been reported to promote the reactivation of TEs. Ageing is among the best-characterised examples. SIRT6 was shown to repress LINE-1 retrotransposons by binding their 5′ UTR and mono-ADP-ribosylating KAP1, thereby promoting heterochromatin formation and transcriptional silencing [53]. This repression was reported to weaken under conditions of stress and ageing, suggesting that age-associated loss of epigenetic control may permit LINE-1 reactivation [53]. In a later study, LINE-1 derepression in senescent cells was associated with accumulation of cytoplasmic LINE-1 cDNA and induction of a type I interferon response, and treatment with the NRTI lamivudine reduced interferon activation and age-associated inflammation in mice [10]. Together, these studies support a model in which age-associated failure of LINE-1 repression may contribute to inflammatory signalling.

    Genetic defects in nucleic acid clearance pathways provide an additional line of evidence linking endogenous retroelements to interferon-driven disease. Mutations in TREX1, which encodes a 3′-5′ DNA exonuclease, were identified as a cause of Aicardi-Goutières syndrome [54]. Subsequent mechanistic work showed that TREX1 deficiency was associated with accumulation of endogenous retroelement-derived DNA, activation of a cell-intrinsic interferon response, and autoimmune pathology [28]. Additionally, it was suggested that TREX1 can suppress LINE-1 activity through additional mechanisms, including depletion of ORF1p, thereby supporting the argument that failure to control retroelement-derived nucleic acids may contribute to chronic inflammatory disease [55]. Infection and other forms of cellular stress have also been proposed as triggers of transposable element reactivation, as discussed in Section 3.

    2.3 Transposable Elements in Autoimmune Disease

    Evidence linking transposable elements (TEs) to autoimmune pathology is now strongest in diseases with prominent interferon signatures, although the quality of evidence varies by condition. In systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), transcriptomic studies have revealed dysregulated expression of LINE-1 and other repetitive elements in patient immune cells, and cGAMP has been detected in a subset of peripheral blood mononuclear cell samples from patients with active disease, consistent with engagement of the cGAS pathway [9, 56]. These findings do not indicate that TE activation is the sole driver of lupus, but they support the idea that endogenous nucleic acids can amplify an already inflamed immune environment.

    The evidence is more heterogeneous in other autoimmune disorders. In rheumatoid arthritis, increased HERV-K (HML-2) expression has been reported in patient samples, and inflammatory cytokines can further increase HERV-K transcription in synovial fibroblasts, suggesting a bidirectional relationship between inflammation and retroelement activity rather than a simple one-way causal model [57]. In multiple sclerosis (MS), HERV-W envelope protein has been detected in active demyelinating lesions, particularly in macrophages and microglia, and experimental work indicates that this envelope protein can impair oligodendroglial precursor differentiation through TLR4-dependent inflammatory signalling [58, 59]. Together, these studies suggest that retroelement products can operate as immunogenic molecules in diseased tissue, but the strength of causal evidence varies across autoimmune conditions.

    Aicardi-Goutières syndrome (AGS) provides the clearest mechanistic link between defective nucleic-acid control, endogenous retroelements, and interferon-mediated disease [7, 8]. Mutations in ADAR1, TREX1 and SAMHD1 each disrupt pathways that normally prevent self-derived nucleic acids from being sensed as danger signals [55, 60]. In a human stem-cell model, TREX1-deficient neurons accumulated LINE-1-derived DNA and developed a neuroinflammatory phenotype, whereas inhibition of reverse transcriptase partially rescued this state [61]. Genetic studies in mice further showed that cGAS is required for the lethal inflammatory phenotype observed in Trex1 deficiency [62]. For this reason, AGS is best understood not simply as a rare paediatric syndrome, but as a high-confidence demonstration that insufficient regulation of endogenous nucleic acids can drive chronic interferonopathy.

    3. The COVID-19 Connection - From Antiviral Response to Autoimmunity

    3.1 Reflecting on My Year 3 Wikiblog

    My original Year 3 Wikiblog addressed SARS-CoV-2 largely based on information available at the time, including acute infection, cytokine dysregulation, and therapeutic approaches such as antivirals, corticosteroids, and JAK inhibitors. What I did not appreciate then was how COVID-19 would raise broader immunological questions about self-versus-non-self nucleic acids, post-viral immune persistence, and the possibility that endogenous retroelements might become relevant during recovery as well as during infection. Revisiting that work now, the main limitation was that it treated viral infection and autoimmunity as adjacent topics rather than as processes that can become mechanistically entangled.

    3.2 SARS-CoV-2 and LINE-1: A Controversial Observation

    One of the most debated claims in this area is that SARS-CoV-2 RNA can undergo LINE-1-mediated reverse transcription and genomic integration in infected cells. Initial reports in cell cultures identified viral-host junctions with several hallmarks of LINE-1 retrotransposition, and a later follow-up study also detected rare retrotransposition events in infected cells, including non-forced LINE-1 overexpression, although the events were much more frequent when LINE-1 was overexpressed experimentally [42, 63]. Therefore, these studies demonstrate that such events can occur under permissive in-vitro conditions.

    However, the interpretation requires caution. The experiments were performed in cell-culture systems, the estimated event frequency was low without exogenous LINE-1 overexpression, and the relevance of these observations in vivo remains unanswered. It is therefore more accurate to treat SARS-CoV-2 integration as a plausible contributor to pathobiology rather than as an established explanation for long COVID. A more confident deduction is that SARS-CoV-2 infection can create a cellular environment rich in inflammation, stress signalling and nucleic-acid sensing, in which endogenous retroelement biology becomes highly relevant.

    3.3 Long COVID and Autoimmune Dysregulation

    Long COVID (post-acute sequelae of SARS-CoV-2 infection, PASC) is better described as a syndrome of overlapping mechanisms than as a single disease entity. A longitudinal multi-omic study identified several early correlates of later PASC, including RNAaemia, Epstein-Barr virus reactivation and specific autoantibodies, implying that persistent symptoms can emerge through more than one immunological route [64]. In a separate cohort, anti-nuclear autoantibodies, including anti-U1-snRNP and anti-SS-B/La, remained associated with fatigue and dyspnoea months after infection [65].

    Nevertheless, it is important to note that TEs have not been established to directly drive long COVID. Instead, long COVID provides a clinical setting in which prolonged inflammatory signalling, autoantibody production, and aberrant nucleic-acid sensing coexist, making the TE-cGAS-STING axis mechanistically relevant as a hypothesis worth further testing.

    3.4 Advances in COVID-19 Therapeutics

    Compared with the therapeutic landscape I described in Year 3, acute COVID-19 management is now more defined than conceptually transformed. Direct antivirals remain most useful early in infection and mainly in patients at higher risk of progression, whereas mechanism-specific therapy for long COVID remains limited [15]. From this Wikiblog’s perspective, that gap is important: even if dysregulated nucleic-acid sensing contributes to post-viral illness in a subset of patients, this insight has not yet translated into validated trials of selective LINE-1 or cGAS-STING-targeted therapy for long COVID. At present, that part of the story remains hypothesis-generating rather than practice-changing.

    4. Innovations in Immunotherapy - From Mechanism to Treatment

    4.1 CAR T-Cell Therapy

    Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy has emerged as a significant recent development in the treatment of severe autoimmune disease. In this setting, the intent is not merely transient immunosuppression but rather profound pathogenic B-cell compartmental depletion, particularly in diseases dominated by autoantibody production. In the initial proof-of-concept study, five patients with refractory SLE achieved marked clinical improvement after anti-CD19 CAR T-cell therapy [16]. A larger follow-up case series extended these observations across refractory SLE, idiopathic inflammatory myositis and systemic sclerosis, with sustained drug-free remissions reported in many patients during follow-up [17]. These results are noteworthy, but they should still be interpreted as early-stage clinical evidence. The published studies are small, uncontrolled and conducted in highly selected patients treated in specialist centres. Nevertheless, they support the idea that in-depth B-cell reprogramming can reset autoimmune activity in at least some patients.

    4.2 Bispecific Antibodies

    Bispecific antibodies represent a conceptually related but still exploratory immunotherapeutic strategy. By simultaneously engaging a B-cell target and CD3 on T cells, they are designed to redirect endogenous cytotoxicity without the customised manufacturing required for CAR T-cell therapy. For example, an early clinical trial in SLE, a phase 1b study, is evaluating the CD20-CD3 bispecific antibody mosunetuzumab in patients with active disease [66]. However, peer-reviewed efficacy data in autoimmune disease remain limited, and bispecific antibodies are therefore mostly described as investigational therapies.

    4.3 JAK Inhibitors - Blocking the Interferon Signal

    JAK inhibitors are highly relevant because they target a signalling pathway downstream of nucleic acid sensing. cGAS-STING activates type I interferon production primarily through TBK1 and IRF3 [4, 51]. However, JAK inhibitors do not block this upstream sensing step, but they diminish the transcriptional programme that follows once interferons and other cytokines engage their receptors [67]. This distinction is crucial because it explains why JAK inhibition can reduce inflammation while the initiating nucleic-acid trigger remains present [67].

    In a phase 2 randomised trial, baricitinib improved skin and joint symptoms in active SLE, supporting the clinical relevance of targeting downstream cytokine signalling in this stage [68]. A phase 1 randomised trial of tofacitinib in SLE further reported safety, along with improvements in immunologic and cardiometabolic parameters, including reductions in the type I interferon gene signature, low-density granulocytes, and circulating NET-associated abnormalities [69]. Together, these studies indicate that JAK inhibition can attenuate interferon-amplified inflammation, but without directly interrupting cGAS-STING activation.

    4.4 STING Inhibitors

    Direct STING inhibition presents a promising approach since it targets the pathway closer to the aberrant activation point. Covalent small-molecule antagonists such as C-176 and related compounds were shown to inhibit activation-induced STING palmitoylation and suppress downstream production of inflammatory cytokines in both mouse and human subjects [13]. SLE-relevant evidence has also emerged, with a STING antagonist peptide, ISD017, reported to block pathological cytokine responses in cells from lupus patients with elevated type I interferon activity and to ameliorate disease development in a mouse lupus model [70]. More recently, ISD017 was reported to reduce glomerulonephritis and improve survival in symptomatic Fcgr2b-deficient mice, supporting the notion that STING inhibition may have therapeutic relevance beyond prevention and into established disease [71]. Overall, STING inhibition appears mechanistically compelling, particularly in conditions dominated by excessive interferon production, but the field remains preclinical.

    4.5 RT Inhibitors - Repurposing Antiretrovirals

    Because LINE-1 encodes its own RT, existing antiretroviral drugs provide a useful means to assess whether retroelement activity contributes to inflammation. In AGS, a pilot study of RT inhibitors reduced interferon signalling, although the clinical signal was modest and the evidence remains preliminary [72]. In ageing models, nucleoside reverse-transcriptase inhibitors reduced LINE-1-associated inflammatory phenotypes [10, 45]. Therefore, these findings propose targeting an upstream trigger rather than suppressing immunity alone, but they have not yet established RT inhibitors as broadly effective treatments for autoimmune disease.

    5. Neuroinflammation

    5.1 cGAS-STING in Neurodegenerative Disease

    The relevance of cGAS-STING extends beyond classic autoimmune disorders. In Alzheimer’s disease (AD) models, cGAS activation has been detected in the brain, and genetic or pharmacological suppression of the pathway reduced amyloid-associated pathology, neuroinflammation and cognitive impairment in 5xFAD mice [73]. In Parkinson’s disease (PD) models, alpha-synuclein pathology activates STING-dependent neuroinflammation, but the best current evidence points to activation of a DNA-damage-linked pathway rather than a simple direct interaction between STING and alpha-synuclein [74]. In amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), TDP-43 pathology can trigger mitochondrial DNA release, cGAS-STING activation and inflammatory injury in motor-neuron systems, with elevated cGAMP also detected in patient spinal cord tissue [75].

    MS sits at the border between autoimmunity and neurodegeneration, making it particularly relevant. HERV-W envelope protein is present in active lesions and can impair oligodendrocyte precursor differentiation through TLR4-dependent mechanisms [58, 59]. However, this does not establish that cGAS-STING is the dominant pathway in MS, but it does exhibit how endogenous retroelement products can contribute directly to inflammatory injury and remyelination failure within the central nervous system.

    5.2 Ageing, LINE-1, and the Brain

    Ageing increases the probability of this entire framework. During senescence and ageing, LINE-1 repression becomes less stable, cytoplasmic LINE-1 cDNA accumulates, and type I interferon-associated inflammatory programmes become more prominent [10, 45]. Nonetheless, this does not indicate that TE activation is implicated in all aspects of brain ageing, but it provides a mechanistically credible route by which age-related epigenetic drift could amplify sterile inflammation in vulnerable tissues.

    5.3 AGS as the Rosetta Stone

    AGS remains the most compelling human model for linking endogenous nucleic acids to neuroinflammation. A human stem-cell study revealed that TREX1 deficiency is sufficient to induce LINE-1 accumulation and neuroinflammatory injury in the nervous system [61], while genetic deletion of cGAS rescues the lethal inflammatory phenotype of Trex1 deficiency in mice [62]. The broader implication is prudent but important: If severe inherited failure of nucleic-acid clearance drives early-life interferonopathy, then milder acquired failures of the same surveillance mechanism may contribute to later-life neuroinflammatory disease.

    6. Reflection and Future Horizons

    Reflective Re-evaluation

    Three Year 3 courses formed the basis of this portfolio, although I did not recognise their intersection at the time. In Molecular Genetics, TEs were presented mainly as genomic entities: LINE-1 structure, target-primed reverse transcription, and the silencing machinery that keeps mobile DNA under control. In Clinical Immunology, autoimmunity was framed primarily in terms of impaired tolerance, apoptosis, or lymphocyte regulation. In Medical Microbiology, antiviral drugs such as nucleoside reverse-transcriptase inhibitors and JAK inhibitors were discussed in the context of infectious diseases. Although each module was individually coherent, I perceived them as parallel topics rather than as parts of a shared molecular system.

    What changed while researching and creating this Wikiblog was not simply discovering more facts, but acquiring a framework that links these subjects together. The cGAS-STING axis provides that framework as it explains how endogenous nucleic acids generated by TEs or defective clearance pathways can be interpreted as signs of infection, how this sustains interferon production independently of exogenous pathogens, and why therapies first encountered in virology or inflammatory medicine may converge mechanistically on the same pathway.

    The most important intellectual shift for me is therefore methodological. I now read immunology less as a set of isolated disease categories and more as a network of molecular events that recur in different tissues and clinical settings. That shift matters because it changes what counts as a meaningful question. Instead of asking only which immune cells are abnormal in a disease, I am now more likely to ask what source of self-derived signal is keeping those cells activated.

    6.2 Future Horizons

    Several future directions emerge from this framework. First, there is biomarker-guided stratification, since not all patients with autoimmune or post-viral syndromes are likely to share the same dominant upstream trigger [29, 76]. Interferon signatures, circulating nucleic-acid markers, and TE expression patterns may eventually help identify which part of the cGAS-STING cascade is most relevant in a given patient and therefore which therapeutic strategy is most appropriate [76, 77].

    Another prospect is that the most effective long-term treatments will be combinatorial rather than single-agent. The therapeutic landscape explored in this Wikiblog suggests that upstream trigger reduction, midstream pathway inhibition, downstream cytokine blockade, and direct targeting of pathogenic immune cells may ultimately need to be combined rather than used in isolation [13, 45, 64, 76]. This would reflect successful multi-target approaches in other fields such as infectious disease and oncology.

    A third priority is to improve understanding of the cGAS-STING axis at the interface between cancer and autoimmunity. Although STING activation can support anti-tumour immunity by inducing type I interferons and amplifying immune effector responses [78, 79], sustained or dysregulated activation may, conversely, promote chronic inflammation, interferon-driven pathology, and autoimmune disease [29, 76]. Defining the molecular and cellular conditions that determine whether cGAS-STING signalling is beneficial or pathogenic will therefore be critical not only for autoimmune research, but also for the design of future cancer immunotherapies and inflammatory disease interventions [18, 78].

    This framework also has implications beyond autoimmunity. The growing links between ageing, TE reactivation, inflammaging, and neuroinflammation suggest that the same molecular machinery may contribute to disease across domains that are usually studied individually [10, 45, 61]. If retroelement-derived nucleic acids help sustain chronic innate immune activation in ageing tissues, then interventions aimed at reducing these upstream triggers may eventually become relevant not only to autoimmune disease, but also to age-associated inflammation and neurodegeneration [10, 45, 61].

    Finally, these ideas could be relevant to post-viral syndromes such as long COVID, although the evidence remains incomplete [63, 64, 77]. If, in at least a subset of patients, persistent immune activation is partially sustained by dysregulated endogenous nucleic-acid sensing or TE activity, then therapeutic strategies targeting these mechanisms would be worth testing [63]. Currently, however, such possibilities remain hypothesis-based rather than clinically established [64, 80].

    The main lesson I take from this research is that the cGAS-STING pathway is a conceptual bridge between genetics, virology, immunology and ageing biology, which has linked different subject modules I have been introduced to over the course of my studies, and has changed how I now think about disease mechanisms in general. I believe that its continued investigation will provide insights not only into autoimmune disease but into the fundamental relationship between our genome’s mobile elements and the immune system that monitors them.
